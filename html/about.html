Basic cuttlefish information

Cuttlefish are cephalopods belonging to the order sepiida, clan cuttle (may or may not be made up), fans of the Tampa Bay Buccaneers (though, to be fair, they mainly just like Tom Brady), and are just plain awesome! You can find cuttlefish in mostly shallow, tropical to temperate waters (can you blame them?) across the globe.

Chromatophores

Cuttlefish are essentially underwater chameleons...well, they're better swimmers, smarter, and not reptiles, BUT they share the color-changing gimmick. Like our poor-swimming friend, the chameleon, cuttlefish have the capacity to cause their skin to change color nearly instantaneously, using specialized, godlike cells called chromatophores. These cells are surrounded by tiny muscles that, when they contract, show the desired pigmentation1.

Shape-Shifters

Like other cephalopods, cuttlefish don't just change colors. No, to become truly camouflaged, cuttlefish can change the texture of their skin by controlling papillae within the skin 2.

30-for-30: Color-blind Ninjas

What if I told you that the greatest master of disguise was color-blind? Crazy as it may seem, cuttlefish are color-blind! So how do they manage to be better at fashion and blending in than all of us non-color-blind noobs? We've already covered chromatophores, but the secret of the cuttlefish camouflage lies in other specialized cells called leucophores, which essentially function as reflectors that can scatter the entire light spectrum and can reflect the filtered light they come across.
